Biodiversity in the Amazon
bi·o·di·ver·si·ty /ˌbīōdiˈvərsədē,ˌbīōˌdīˈvərsədē/

(noun) The variety of life in the world or in a particular habitat or ecosystem.

The Amazon covers 2.124 million square miles (40% of the world’s remaining rainforest) and is home incredibly vibrant ecosystem, hosting over 3 million species and over 2,500 tree species (25% of the world’s biodiversity).

SPOTLIGHT - Milton’s Titi (Plecturocebus miltoni)

First catalogued in 2014, its habitat is bounded by the Roosevelt and Aripuanã rivers in the states of Mato Grosso and Amazonas, Brazil. The deforestation activities advancing from the south have left the monkeys fewer places to live with each passing day. Because it lives exclusively in the treetops, the loss of forest cover is fatal for this species. Its main threats are fires and forest degradation.
