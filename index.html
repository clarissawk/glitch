Biodiversity in the Amazon
bi·o·di·ver·si·ty /ˌbīōdiˈvərsədē,ˌbīōˌdīˈvərsədē/

(noun) The variety of life in the world or in a particular habitat or ecosystem.

The Amazon is a sprawling rainforest in Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Bolivia, Guyana, Suriname and French Guiana

Covering 2.124 million square miles (40% of the world’s remaining rainforest) it is the world's largest rainforest and key part of the globl carbon and oxygen system. Is is an incredibly vibrant ecosystem, hosting over 3 million species and over 2,500 tree species (25% of the world’s biodiversity).

Cattle Farming and its Effects

Cattle farming is the largest driver behind deforestation in the Amazon. Ranchers raze forest to the ground to provide grazing for their stock. We are already seeing species dwindle due to habitat and food loss.

SPOTLIGHT - Milton’s Titi (Plecturocebus miltoni)

First catalogued in 2014, its habitat is bounded by the Roosevelt and Aripuanã rivers in the states of Mato Grosso and Amazonas, Brazil. The deforestation activities advancing from the south are devastating its habitat. Because it lives exclusively in the treetops, the loss of forest cover is fatal for this species. Its main threats are fires and forest degradation.
